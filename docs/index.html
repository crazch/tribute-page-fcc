Alan Turing

The Man Who Thought Before Machines Could

Alan Mathison Turing (/ˈtjʊərɪŋ/; 23 June 1912 – 7 June 1954) was an English mathematician, computer scientist, logician, cryptanalyst, philosopher and theoretical biologist.[5] He was highly influential in the development of theoretical computer science, providing a formalisation of the concepts of algorithm and computation with the Turing machine, which can be considered a model of a general-purpose computer.[6][7][8] Turing is widely considered to be the father of theoretical computer science. (Source: Wikipedia)

Biography Timeline

  • 1912 – Birth Born on June 23 in Maida Vale, London, England.
  • 1936 – Turing Machine Concept Published "On Computable Numbers", introducing the idea of the Turing Machine, a theoretical model for computing.
  • 1939 – Joins Bletchley Park Started working for British intelligence to help decrypt Nazi codes during WWII.
  • 1940 – Begins Cracking Enigma Led the team that built the Bombe machine, successfully deciphering Enigma-encrypted messages.
  • 1945 – War Ends, Receives OBE Awarded the Order of the British Empire (OBE) for his secret wartime service.
  • 1952 – Arrest and Persecution Prosecuted for homosexuality, which was illegal in the UK at the time. Chose chemical castration over prison.
  • 1954 – Death Died on June 7 from cyanide poisoning. Widely considered suicide, though some speculate it may have been accidental.
